At the beginning of the twenty-first century, the internet gave birth to the open movement. Numerous organisations and initiatives have been launched with a belief in openness and free knowledge. Their proponents placed their bets on the combined power of networked information services and new governance models for the production and sharing of content and data. We – as members of this broad movement – were among those who believed it possible to leverage this combination of power and opportunity to build a more democratic society, unleashing the power of the internet to create universal access to knowledge and culture. For us, such openness meant not only freedom, but also presented a path to justice and equality.1

1 Open Future Foundation (2021), the Paradox of Open.

The open revolution that did not happen.

The open revolution that we2 imagined did not happen. At least not on the scale that we and many other proponents of free culture expected. Wikipedia and the many open source software (OSS) projects that power the infrastructure of the internet have shown that the success of the open movement and demonstrated that these ideas and ideals can create significant value for the common good. Yet, over the last decade we have witnessed a wholesale transformation of the networked information ecosystem. The internet moved away from the ideals and the open design of the early internet and turned into an environment that is dominated by a small number of platforms.

The trend away from openess to closed, proprietary systems is also at hand in the area of data infrastructure and engineering. What started of with the ‘Big Data’ boom at the turn of the millenium, has by evolved into one of the largest growth areas of digital infrastructure, estimated at a market worth over 70 billion US dollars by 2022.3 The push for commercial return-on-investment has led to a proliferation of technologies and products which makes it hard for companies and organisation to make well-informed decisions as to which data platform is best suitable for them. Furthermore, what is a fair price that companies need to pay for their data platform? From our own experience, we see organizations spending up to 2% of their revenue on data-related infrastructure, which we believe is an unneccessary burden particularly for public and non-profit organisations such as healthcare providers and government organizations.

As advocates of openness, we have largely failed to take into account the negative externalities related to the permissive sharing of all kinds of information, including the data itself and the OSS that power data platforms. Today, this situation contributes to the power imbalances that we observe. Companies like OpenAI and Anthropic could not have created their foundational large language models (LLMs) without Wikipedia, yet Wikipedia is struggling to make ends meet. Key OSS projects are severly underfunded leading to, amongst other, serious cybersecurity vulnerabilities. Most, if not all, commercial data infrastructure providers use the same core of OSS libraries in their commercial platform products, yet we see increasing lock-in and vendor dependency. Is there a viable strategy to counter this trend?

Open source as a strategy for reclaiming sovereignty.

We believe that open source provides a viable strategy to rebalance the power over digital platform and reclaim sovereingty.4 Over the past decades, we have seen public and private actors wield this tool to neutralize monopolistic control, commoditize critical digital infrastructure, and realign industries around open, collectively governed standards. Looking at how Google used Android to prevent Apple’s dominance, Meta’s formation of the Open Compute Project and Google’s strategy with Kubernetes, we see that open source has repeatedly served as a lever to remove supplier lock-in and redistribute power. Countries like China have gone further, adopting open ecosystems as part of national strategy, using open standards and shared platforms to strengthen technological autonomy and resilience. If we are to reclaim sovereignty and flexibility in digital infrastructure, our approach to openness must mirror these strategic uses: not just releasing resources, but building and governing them in ways that limit dependence on private gatekeepers and maximize collective benefit.

We have started SRDP to operationalize this strategy, where we specifically address some of the issues related to working with open source in the context of data platforms these o reclaiming openess, sovereignity and flexibility for our data and data platforms

Theme Challenge Our approach
Lack of system integration While there are many mature and robust open source components for data platforms, we lack a trusted and collaborative approach to systems integration of these components. Use-of-use is one of the key USPs of commercial vendors. Taking inspiration from Instant OpenHIE, an initiative that aims to allow Health Information Exchange components to be packaged up, deployed, operated and scaled via a simple CLI, we envision SRDP as tool do the same for dataplatforms based on the lakehouse architecture.
Lack of trust in the open source ecosystem No one got fired your buying . And indeed we acknowledge that trust is as important as the technology itself. Many open source based service providers do not have the scale and scope to provide all the things required for a robust, well-governed data platform We envisage SRDP Hub as a cooperative of like-minded professionals and suppliers, such that as a group we can provide the full range of services required to build and operate a data platform, while at the same time providing a trusted network for our customers. We aim to launch a certification programme, through a peer-review mechanism where partners in the ecosystem evaluate each other’s solution. In future, this could be extended in, for example, having a managed PyPI mirror (rather than every organization setting up their own secure mirror). The cooperative is governed by a Holacracy, which is a decentralized organizational structure that distributes authority and decision-making across self-organizing teams. This way of working allows us to be more agile and responsive to the needs of our customers, while also fostering a culture of collaboration and shared ownership.
